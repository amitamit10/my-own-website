I did not want this portfolio to feel like a clean template with my name dropped into it.

The whole point was to make the site itself part of the work. Instead of treating branding as something I would solve later with a logo and a color picker, I wanted the identity system to come from a real physical process. That is what led to the photographed water letters, the custom font pipeline, and eventually the wordmarks that anchor the site.

## Starting from material instead of trend

Most portfolio sites start from interface references. This one started from a material.

I built a small phone capture tool to photograph water shaped into letters, remove the background, and save the glyphs as transparent PNGs. After the capture pass, I cleaned the set, assembled a font locally, and generated image-based wordmarks from the real photographed letters.

That changed the rest of the design immediately. Once the font existed, the site could not behave like a generic product landing page anymore. The palette had to come from the letters. The motion had to feel tactile. The supporting layout had to help the wordmarks feel intentional instead of ornamental.

## The main design problem

The hardest part was not the font itself. The hard part was keeping the page readable.

If I had used the water treatment everywhere, the site would have become exhausting fast. If I used it too little, then the visual identity would feel fake. So the real design challenge was deciding where the handmade system should speak loudly and where it should get out of the way.

I ended up using the photographed wordmarks as anchors, then built the rest of the page around them with paper stacks, rough frames, taped notes, and low-FPS motion. The body copy stays clean. The structure stays legible. The expressive parts are concentrated where they matter most.

## Making motion feel less digital

I did not want buttery transitions or polished floating cards. That kind of movement would fight the whole idea.

So the motion language became intentionally stepped:

- frame-like jitter on key wordmarks
- subtle drift on doodles and collage pieces
- small hover nudges instead of smooth transform-heavy animation
- a slightly misregistered feel in the supporting paper layers

That low-FPS treatment makes the page feel closer to stop-motion than to a default modern UI demo.

## What I am happiest with

I am happiest that the system still works as a portfolio first.

The site has personality, but it is not hiding the work behind personality. Projects still read clearly. The GitHub section still feels like real information instead of decoration. Even the empty and fallback states now belong to the same world.

That balance matters to me. I like interfaces that have taste, but I like them even more when the taste does not get in the way of understanding.

## What comes next

The next layer is turning the blog flow into something real, not just visually ready.

The route now exists and matches the rest of the site, but the longer-term plan is to generate project write-ups through a lightweight AI-assisted workflow, review them manually, and publish only the ones that feel honest enough to keep.

That feels consistent with the whole project: use automation where it helps, but keep the human taste in the loop.
